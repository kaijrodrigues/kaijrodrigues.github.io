RPG Concept Test: Proposed Survey Design for a Major Gaming Studio

Concept Testing Survey Design Conjoint 2026

Overview

A take-home case study completed as part of a research role interview at the insights team of a major gaming company. The brief had two parts: pull insights from a profiling dataset, and propose a survey structure for an upcoming RPG concept test.

This page covers the survey design deliverable. The research team had already aligned on objectives and methodology with the game team. The task was to translate four research goals into a concrete survey structure with sample design, section flow, and skip logic.

Research Brief

The studio was exploring a new RPG title for PC and console and needed a quantitative study to evaluate the concept with players in North America and Europe. Four research goals were given:

  • Audience Understanding: Identify the primary and secondary audience; surface habits, motivations, and attitudes
  • Concept Resonance: Evaluate overall appeal, standout elements, weak elements, and barriers
  • Expectations: Assess genre alignment and competitive overlap with titles players are currently playing
  • Positioning Inputs: Identify features and themes that could strengthen market fit; flag whether follow-up research is needed

Methodology was pre-specified: a quantitative online survey, 10–15 minute LOI, targeting NA + EU RPG players on PC or console.

Sample Design

The proposed sample is N=1,000, split evenly between North America and Europe. Within each region, 60% are RPG players and 40% are not. The non-RPG segment is intentional: concept appeal outside the core audience is a meaningful signal of expansion potential.

  • North America: n=300 RPG players · n=200 non-RPG players
  • Europe: n=300 RPG players · n=200 non-RPG players
  • Minimum n=200 per segment keeps conjoint utilities reliable at the subgroup level
  • Screener quotas enforce the RPG/non-RPG split; RPG over-recruitment is capped to avoid panel bias

Survey Flow

I proposed a four-section structure ordered to minimize respondent fatigue, with stimulus exposure and conjoint placed before U&A to avoid priming attitudinal responses.

Fig. 1: Proposed survey flow: Screener → Concept Test → Conjoint → Usage & Attitudes.

  • Screener: Age, gender, region, platform use, weekly playtime, and genres played in the past 6 months. Non-qualifying respondents are terminated early; the RPG/non-RPG quota is enforced here.
  • Concept Test: Stimulus exposure followed by appeal, purchase intent, and uniqueness ratings. Element-level diagnostics cover gameplay, story, and visual style, with the diagnostic and rating blocks randomized. RPG players get additional questions on genre alignment and competitive overlap.
  • Conjoint Analysis: 10 choice tasks; respondents pick a preferred game profile from 3 options or indicate none appeals. Profile composition is randomized across tasks.
  • Usage & Attitudes: Placed last to prevent priming. Covers franchises played, spending habits, and gaming motivations. Non-RPG players skip the RPG-specific habit questions.

Conjoint Design

Conjoint analysis was proposed to identify which feature combinations drive preference and pressure-test internal tradeoffs, particularly around pricing model and social content structure where the studio had competing design directions.

Attribute Levels
Setting High fantasy  ·  Sci-fi fantasy  ·  Post-apocalyptic
Pricing Model Free trial + monthly subscription  ·  One-time purchase + monthly subscription  ·  One-time purchase + paid expansions
In-Game Activities Challenging group raids  ·  Player economy with crafting/trading  ·  PvP and competitive play
Social Content Solo-first with optional group play  ·  Balanced solo and group content  ·  Group-focused progression
