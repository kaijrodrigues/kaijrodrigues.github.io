RPG Concept Test: Proposed Survey Design for a Major Gaming Studio

Concept Testing Survey Design Conjoint 2026

Overview

A major gaming studio was evaluating candidates for an Associate Researcher role on their Consumer & Market Insights team. The take-home case study included two tasks: generating audience insights from a profiling dataset, and proposing a full survey structure for an upcoming RPG concept test.

This page covers the survey design deliverable. The brief specified that the research team had already aligned on objectives and methodology with the game team — the task was to translate four research goals into a concrete, justified survey structure with sample design, section flow, and skip logic.

Research Brief

The studio was exploring a new RPG title for PC and console and needed a quantitative study to evaluate the concept with their target audience in North America and Europe. The research had four stated goals:

  • Audience Understanding: Identify the primary and secondary audience for the concept; surface habits, motivations, and attitudes
  • Concept Resonance: Evaluate overall appeal, standout elements, weak elements, and barriers
  • Expectations: Assess whether the concept aligns with what RPG players expect from the genre, and how it competes with titles they currently play
  • Positioning Inputs: Identify which features and themes could strengthen market fit; determine whether follow-up research is needed

Methodology was pre-specified: a quantitative online survey, 10–15 minute estimated LOI, targeting NA + EU RPG players on PC or console.

Survey Flow

I proposed a four-section structure ordered to minimize respondent fatigue and maximize data quality — stimulus exposure and conjoint placed before U&A to avoid priming effects on attitudinal questions.

Fig. 1 — Proposed survey flow: Screener → Concept Test → Conjoint → Usage & Attitudes.

  • Screener: Age, gender, region, platform use, weekly playtime, and genres played in the past 6 months. Non-qualifying respondents are terminated early. RPG vs. non-RPG split is enforced at quota.
  • Concept Test: Stimulus exposure followed by appeal, purchase intent, and uniqueness ratings. Element-level diagnostics (gameplay, story, visual style) are then evaluated with the diagnostic and element blocks randomized across respondents. RPG players receive additional questions on genre alignment and competitive overlap.
  • Conjoint: 10 choice tasks; respondents select a preferred game profile from 3 options or indicate none appeals. Profile composition is randomized across tasks.
  • Usage & Attitudes: Placed last to prevent priming. Covers franchises played, spending habits, and gaming motivations. Non-RPG players skip the RPG-specific habit module.

Conjoint Design

A discrete choice conjoint was proposed to identify which feature combinations drive preference and to simulate internal tradeoff scenarios — particularly around pricing model and social content structure, where the studio faced competing design directions.

Attribute Levels
Setting High fantasy  ·  Sci-fi fantasy  ·  Post-apocalyptic
Pricing Model Free trial + monthly subscription  ·  One-time purchase + monthly subscription  ·  One-time purchase + paid expansions
In-Game Activities Challenging group raids  ·  Player economy with crafting/trading  ·  PvP and competitive play
Social Content Solo-first with optional group play  ·  Balanced solo and group content  ·  Group-focused progression

Sample Design

I proposed a total sample of N=1,000, split evenly across regions and with a 60/40 RPG-to-non-RPG ratio within each. The non-RPG segment was included deliberately — concept appeal among adjacent-genre players signals expansion potential beyond the core audience.

  • North America: n=300 RPG players · n=200 non-RPG players
  • Europe: n=300 RPG players · n=200 non-RPG players
  • Minimum n=200 per segment ensures reliable conjoint utilities at the subgroup level
  • Screener quotas enforce the RPG/non-RPG split; over-recruitment on RPG players is capped to avoid panel bias
